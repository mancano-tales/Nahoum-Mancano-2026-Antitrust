Antitrust as industrial policy: Government-Sponsored Mergers as Passive
Industrial Policy in Brazil, 1995-2002

André Vereta-Nahoum

Tales Mançano

## 1. Introduction

Can governments pursue industrial policy while explicitly denying doing
so? This article examines how Brazil's antitrust enforcement in the
1990s operated as a form of industrial policy under conditions of fiscal
constraint and liberalizing reform, despite the official rejection of
such policy by leading policymakers. During the administrations of
Fernando Henrique Cardoso, economic policy was publicly organized around
macroeconomic stabilization, market liberalization, and a commitment to
limiting state intervention in markets. As the then Finance Minister
Pedro Malan put it, "the best industrial policy is to have no industrial
policy" [\[ADD EXACT SOURCE\].]{.mark} Yet, at the same time, major
mergers involving leading Brazilian firms were approved with explicit
support from the executive branch and justified in terms of the need to
compete internationally. This apparent contradiction constitutes the
central puzzle of the paper. [In the absence and even explicital denial
of specific industrial policy mechanisms, antitrust can serve as an
indirect regulatory means with similar effects of benefitting specific
firms and industries with firm-level competitiveness or sectoral
concentration. In this sense, industrial policy may operate not through
visible interventions such as subsidies or protection, but through the
reinterpretation and selective enforcement of existing legal frameworks.
This case also emphasizes the importance of analytically disentangling
state discourses, policy instruments, and policy effects.]{.mark}

Passive industrial policy, as defined in this article, refers to the
promotion of sectoral or firm-level competitiveness through regulatory
practices that do not involve fiscal transfers or direct state
coordination, but instead operate by enabling market restructuring. When
such policies are pursued under explicit discursive denial, they take
the form of covert industrial policy. This passivity should not be
mistaken for state absence. Rather, it reflects a form of indirect and
strategically mediated intervention, in which the state shapes market
outcomes by exercising discretion over enforcement, interpretation, and
institutional design. In the Brazilian case, this occurred through the
lenient application of antitrust law, which allowed firms to consolidate
their domestic market positions and subsequently expand internationally.

The mechanism through which this process unfolds can be understood as a
form of institutional conversion, in the sense developed by Mahoney and
Thelen (2010). Existing rules are not formally changed but are
reinterpreted in ways that produce outcomes that diverge from their
original intent. In the context of antitrust policy, two legal concepts
are particularly central to this process: efficiency and the definition
of the relevant market. While efficiency was formally tied to consumer
welfare in the new competition regime, it could be expanded in practice
to include considerations of firm competitiveness and technological
upgrading. More importantly, the concept of the relevant market proved
highly malleable. By defining markets as global rather than national,
actors were able to dilute measures of concentration and justify mergers
as necessary responses to international competition. This ambiguity
created room for interpretation that enabled the conversion of antitrust
policy into an instrument of industrial policy.

The empirical analysis is based on a comparative study of two emblematic
merger cases in Brazil during the 1990s: the acquisition of Pains by the
Gerdau Group in the steel sector and the merger of Antarctica and Brahma
into Ambev in the beverage sector. These cases are particularly
revealing because they were highly contested, involved extensive
documentation, and generated significant political debate. The study
employs process tracing based on administrative rulings by CADE,
technical reports produced by government agencies, corporate statements,
and media coverage. In order to manage and analyze a large volume of
textual material, the research also relied on computational assistance
using large language models, namely NotebookLM. These tools were used to
reconstruct procedural timelines, identify recurring argumentative
patterns, and compare the positions of different actors across
documents. Their use was strictly supportive, facilitating the
organization and interrogation of the material, while all analytical
interpretations were carried out by the authors.

The Brazilian case is especially illuminating because it unfolds in a
context where industrial policy was not only absent but explicitly
disavowed. Following the enactment of the 1994 competition law, the
Administrative Council for Economic Defense (CADE) was strengthened as
an autonomous authority tasked with promoting competition and supporting
macroeconomic stabilization. The broader policy framework emphasized the
reduction of state intervention and the disciplining of markets, in part
as a strategy to control inflation. At the same time, trade
liberalization exposed domestic firms to increased foreign competition,
raising concerns about their capacity to survive and expand in a more
open economy. It is within this context that antitrust policy became a
site of strategic reinterpretation.

Taken together, the two cases reveal the process of institutional
conversion that allowed the manipulation of antitrust regulatory
principles to serve as a passive industrial policy. In the Gerdau case,
the reinterpretation of antitrust principles required direct political
intervention and generated visible conflict within the regulatory
framework. By the time of the Ambev merger, similar interpretive moves
were more readily accommodated within the logic of the competition
regime itself. The ambiguity surrounding the definition of the relevant
market played a central role in this transformation, providing the legal
and analytical flexibility necessary to reconcile high levels of
domestic concentration with the formal requirements of competition law.
Through this process, antitrust policy came to function as a mechanism
for enabling firm consolidation and promoting international
competitiveness, even as industrial policy continued to be publicly
disavowed.

These findings have broader implications for debates on industrial
policy and competition in international political economy. They suggest
that industrial policy need not take the form of explicit intervention
or fiscal expenditure, but can be embedded in the operation of
regulatory regimes typically associated with market discipline. They
also challenge the view of antitrust as a neutral and purely
technocratic instrument, highlighting its susceptibility to political
reinterpretation and strategic use. Finally, the Brazilian case
resonates with contemporary debates in the United States and the
European Union, where policymakers have increasingly sought to reconcile
antitrust enforcement with the need to foster globally competitive firms
in sectors such as digital platforms, artificial intelligence, and green
technologies. These debates are often framed as a choice between
maintaining strict competition or relaxing enforcement to enable scale
[(Khan 2017; Juhász, Lane, and Rodrik 2023).]{.mark}

The Brazilian case suggests that this dichotomy may be misleading.
Rather than formally suspending antitrust principles, states may pursue
industrial objectives through the internal reinterpretation of
competition law, allowing concentration while preserving the formal
language of market efficiency and consumer welfare. In this sense,
contemporary efforts to align antitrust with industrial strategy may
already involve forms of covert and passive industrial policy, even
where such policies are not explicitly acknowledged.

within existing debates on antitrust and industrial policy. The
subsequent sections analyze the Gerdau and Ambev cases in detail,
followed by a comparative discussion of the mechanisms at work and their
broader implications.

## 2. Antitrust and Industrial Policy: Reframing the Relationship

The relationship between antitrust and industrial policy has long been
framed as one of tension, if not outright incompatibility. Early
antitrust traditions in the United States, associated with Louis
Brandeis and progressive-era reformers, viewed concentration as both an
economic and political threat, opposing large-scale corporate
organization on the grounds that it undermined democracy and fair
competition (Berk 1994; Hovenkamp 2005). Within this perspective,
industrial policy---understood as state support for specific sectors or
firms---appeared as a dangerous extension of concentrated power, further
entrenching dominant actors at the expense of smaller competitors.

This skepticism persisted, albeit in different form, within the Chicago
School and transaction-cost approaches that came to dominate antitrust
thinking in the late twentieth century. While these approaches rejected
Brandeisian hostility to large firms, they nonetheless opposed
industrial policy interventions on the grounds that they distorted
market signals and reduced efficiency (Posner 1979; Bork 1978;
Williamson 1975). Antitrust, in this view, should focus narrowly on
consumer welfare, allowing concentration where it produces efficiency
gains and intervening only where clear harm can be demonstrated.

In recent years, the resurgence of industrial policy in advanced
economies has reopened these debates. Scholars and policymakers have
increasingly questioned whether strict adherence to competition
principles is compatible with the need to foster innovation, build
strategic industries, and compete in global markets (Khan 2017; Rodrik
2021; Juhász, Lane, and Rodrik 2023). Yet even in this renewed context,
the relationship between antitrust and industrial policy is often framed
as a trade-off, with governments facing a choice between maintaining
competitive markets and enabling the emergence of large, globally
competitive firms.

This binary framing obscures the possibility that antitrust itself may
serve as a vehicle for industrial policy. Historical institutionalist
accounts have pointed in this direction, showing that regulatory regimes
can shape market structures in ways that resemble industrial policy
outcomes, even in the absence of explicit state planning (Dobbin 1994;
Prasad 2012). However, these accounts have focused primarily on earlier
periods and have not systematically examined how such processes unfold
in contemporary liberalizing contexts.

The Brazilian case suggests that the opposition between antitrust and
industrial policy is not only overstated but analytically misleading.
Rather than choosing between competition and intervention, governments
may pursue industrial objectives through the internal reinterpretation
of competition law. This requires shifting attention away from formal
policy categories and toward the ways in which legal concepts are
mobilized and redefined in practice. In particular, it calls for
examining how ambiguities embedded in antitrust frameworks---such as
those surrounding efficiency and market definition---create
opportunities for strategic reinterpretation.

By foregrounding these dynamics, the paper shows that antitrust policy,
far from being a neutral or purely technical domain, is deeply embedded
in broader struggles over economic strategy and state intervention. In
doing so, it opens up a new perspective on the relationship between
liberalization and industrial policy, suggesting that the former may not
displace the latter but rather transform its modalities.

## 3. Context: Liberalization, Institutional Reform, and the Emergence of a New Antitrust Regime

The transformation of Brazil's political economy in the 1990s provides a
critical backdrop for understanding the cases analyzed in this paper.
Following decades of state-led development characterized by import
substitution, state-owned enterprises, and corporatist arrangements, the
country embarked on a process of economic liberalization that reshaped
the relationship between state and market. Trade barriers were reduced,
privatization programs were implemented, and macroeconomic stabilization
became the central objective of economic policy.

Within this broader transformation, the reform of the competition regime
played a key role. The enactment of the 1994 antitrust law strengthened
the institutional capacity and autonomy of CADE, positioning it as the
central authority responsible for enforcing competition policy. The new
framework aimed not only to promote efficiency and consumer welfare but
also to support macroeconomic stabilization by preventing price
distortions and anti-competitive practices that could undermine
inflation control.

At the same time, the ideological orientation of the government
emphasized a retreat from direct state intervention in the economy.
Policymakers sought to distance themselves from the developmentalist
strategies of previous decades, framing liberalization as both a
technical necessity and a normative commitment to market principles.
This orientation was reflected in public statements that rejected
industrial policy and in efforts to limit the role of the state in
coordinating economic activity.

Yet this apparent withdrawal of the state coincided with new challenges.
The opening of the economy exposed domestic firms to increased
competition from foreign producers, raising concerns about their ability
to survive and expand in a more integrated global market. In this
context, the consolidation of domestic firms emerged as a potential
strategy for enhancing competitiveness, even as the formal policy
framework discouraged direct intervention.

It is within this tension between ideological commitment and economic
necessity that the reinterpretation of antitrust policy took place. The
new competition regime, while designed to promote market discipline,
also contained ambiguities that could be mobilized to accommodate
consolidation. The following sections examine how these ambiguities were
exploited in practice through the Gerdau and Ambev cases.

## 4. The Gerdau--Pains Case: Conflict, Intervention, and the Reinterpretation of Antitrust

The Gerdau--Pains case provides an early and revealing instance of the
tensions inherent in the new competition regime. In the mid-1990s,
Gerdau's acquisition of assets associated with the German group Korf,
including the Pains steel mill, raised concerns within CADE about
excessive concentration in the domestic market for long steel products,
particularly rebar. Central to the dispute were the definition of the
relevant market and the assessment of potential [efficiency gains, which

increased market concentration.]{.mark} [(explain efficiency and include
article of the law)]{.mark} The case unfolded over a protracted period
marked by legal disputes, political intervention, and institutional
transformation.

In its initial assessment, CADE adopted a relatively strict
interpretation of competition principles. The authority emphasized that
the merger would substantially increase concentration in the domestic
market and questioned the validity of the efficiency claims advanced by
the firms. CADE's technical analysis suggested that, despite trade
liberalization, domestic prices for steel products remained largely
decoupled from international benchmarks, indicating that the market
retained a predominantly national character. This finding implied that
the merger would create significant market power within Brazil, raising
concerns about potential anti-competitive effects. [(citar
processo)]{.mark}

The authority initially issued a partial approval, requiring the
deconstitution of part of the operation. Gerdau and its allies contested
this interpretation, arguing that steel should be understood as a global
commodity subject to international competition. By framing the relevant
market in global terms, they sought to demonstrate that domestic
concentration was less problematic and that the merger was necessary to
achieve the scale required to compete internationally [(citar
processo).]{.mark}

The firms challenged the decision, refused to comply, and appealed to
the Minister of Justice, arguing that the legal framework granting CADE
final authority was unconstitutional. The Minister accepted the appeal
and suspended enforcement, effectively intervening in the process. The
argument raised by Gerdau's legal team was supported by broader claims
about the need for consolidation in the context of globalization, with
political actors assuming steel is a global commodity and emphasizing
that mergers and acquisitions among Brazilian firms in the industry were

CARDOSO OR MINISTER\]]{.mark}

The case was then reconsidered by a reconfigured CADE, which ultimately
approved the acquisition with conditions, including divestments and
commitments regarding supply and designed to mitigate anti-competitive
effects. However, the outcome of the case extended beyond the specific
operation. It had significant institutional consequences and contributed
to the redefinition of antitrust policy in Brazil. In response to the
tensions generated by the decision, President Cardoso altered the
composition of CADE, replacing members with legal backgrounds [from the
AGU]{.mark} with economists trained in transaction-cost approaches to
antitrust [(Onto)]{.mark}. [fala de FHC -- não causem

This shift in expertise contributed to a reorientation of the authority
toward a more permissive stance regarding mergers and acquisitions,
emphasizing efficiency and conduct remedies over structural constraints.
The case established a precedent for the acceptance of mergers justified
in terms of international competitiveness. This transformation can be
understood as part of a broader process of institutional conversion
[(Mahoney & Thelen)]{.mark}, in which the meaning and application of
competition law were reshaped without formal legislative change.

## 5. The Ambev Case: Consolidation, Legitimization, and the Stabilization of Conversion

The Ambev case, decided at the end of the decade, reflects the
consolidation of this new orientation. The merger between Antarctica and
Brahma, announced in 1999, brought together the two largest beer
producers in the country, creating a firm that controlled approximately
70 percent of the beer market and a substantial share of distribution
networks.

From the outset, the merger was framed by its proponents in terms that
went beyond conventional efficiency arguments.

The operation was announced by its proponents as essential for the
creation of a "green-and-yellow multinational" capable of competing with
global industry leaders and warned that failure to achieve sufficient
scale would result in the denationalization of the sector. \[[INSERT
QUOTE FROM EXECUTIVES\]]{.mark}. This framing resonated with political
actors, who publicly endorsed the merger as part of a broader strategy
to strengthen domestic firms in the face of international competition.
[In fact, the operation was announced with President Cardoso...]{.mark}

The procedural trajectory of the case reveals both continuity and change
relative to the Gerdau case. Following the announcement of the merger in
July 1999, CADE issued a precautionary measure suspending its
implementation pending review. Technical bodies within the government,
including the Secretary for Economic Monitoring, challenged the
efficiency claims advanced by the firms, arguing that projected cost
reductions were overstated and unlikely to benefit consumers. At the
same time, debates over the definition of the relevant market reemerged,
with the firms advocating for a broad definition encompassing all
beverages, while critics insisted on focusing on the beer market, where
concentration was extreme and exports remained minimal.

Despite these concerns, the executive branch strongly supported the
merger, with the Ministry of Development affirming its alignment with
national interests [(citação do processo)]{.mark}. Public statements by
President Cardoso suggested a pragmatic acceptance of market
concentration, encapsulated in the observation that "the world operates
in oligopolies" [\[ADD SOURCE\].]{.mark} This position signaled a shift
away from strict adherence to competition principles and toward a more
flexible approach that accommodated the realities of global markets.

In March 2000, CADE approved the merger subject to a set of conditions
formalized in a Performance Commitment Term. These conditions included
divestments, measures to ensure access to distribution networks, and
commitments to maintain employment levels. While these remedies were
intended to address competition concerns, they also reflected a broader
acceptance of the merger's underlying rationale.

The Ambev case thus illustrates the stabilization of the interpretive
shifts that had emerged in the Gerdau case. The ambiguity surrounding
market definition and efficiency was no longer a source of acute
conflict but had become part of the routine operation of the competition
regime. Antitrust policy, in this context, functioned as a mechanism for
enabling consolidation while maintaining the formal language of
competition and consumer welfare.

## 6. Discussion: Institutional Conversion and the Reconfiguration of State Intervention

The comparative analysis of the Gerdau and Ambev cases reveals a process
of institutional conversion in which the meaning and application of
antitrust policy were transformed without formal legislative change.
Central to this process was the strategic use of ambiguity in key legal
concepts, particularly the definition of the relevant market. By
expanding the scope of the market to include international competition,
actors were able to reconcile high levels of domestic concentration with
the requirements of competition law.

This reinterpretation was facilitated by political intervention and
institutional change, including the reconfiguration of CADE's
composition and the increasing prominence of economic expertise aligned
with transaction-cost approaches. Over time, these changes contributed
to the normalization of a more permissive stance toward mergers, in
which concerns about market structure were balanced against
considerations of efficiency and competitiveness.

The result was a form of industrial policy that operated through passive
regulatory means and was justified in covert terms. While the government
continued to reject industrial policy in its discourse, it enabled the
consolidation of domestic firms and their expansion into international
markets. This suggests that liberalization does not necessarily entail a
retreat of the state but may instead involve a reconfiguration of its
modes of intervention.

## 7. Conclusion

This paper has argued that antitrust policy in Brazil during the 1990s
functioned as a form of covert industrial policy implemented through
passive regulatory means. By enabling firm consolidation through the
reinterpretation of legal concepts and the selective enforcement of
competition law, the state fostered the emergence of nationally rooted
multinational firms without resorting to traditional industrial policy
tools.

These findings have important implications for the study of industrial
policy and competition in international political economy. They
highlight the need to move beyond dichotomous frameworks that oppose
state intervention and market discipline and to consider the ways in
which regulatory regimes can be repurposed to achieve developmental
objectives. They also suggest that contemporary debates about the
relationship between antitrust and industrial policy, particularly in
advanced economies, may benefit from greater attention to the covert and
passive forms that such policies can take.

By disaggregating discourse, instruments, and effects, the paper
provides a framework for analyzing these dynamics and contributes to a
more nuanced understanding of the role of the state in shaping market
outcomes under conditions of liberalization.
